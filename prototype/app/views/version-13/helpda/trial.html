Trial and court

Preparing for trial

Being at trial/court

If there is enough evidence to charge the abuser with a criminal offense the case will go to trial. The trial will either be in a Magistrates Court heard by a judge, or a Crown Court for a trial by jury. Depending on the type of trial or court your case is assigned to you there will be more preparations or things to consider.

When you get the trial or hearing date always check the address of the Court, as it may not be the closet court to you. It’s worth looking into how you would get to court as soon as the date is confirmed as the Witness Care Unit can help you if you are likely to run into any difficulties with the travel.

Witness expenses also include Victim’s and are available for your travel costs, as well as childcare and renumeration if your employer does not give you the day off work.

If you cannot make the trial date you must let the Witness Care Unit know as soon as possible.

What signifies this stage?

[When a case is ready to go to trial you can hear from several organisations in a short period of time. Your investigating officer will often be the person to update you on the case progression, however you may also:

  • Have received a letter with a fixed date or a 'warned period' to attend the Court either remotely or in person.
  • A representative from the Police Witness Care Unit (a Witness Care Officer) has been in touch with you to help you prepare to give evidence
  • You have been referred to the Citizens Advice Witness Service

What can you do?

[It’s normal to feel nervous before a trial, even if you’ve been waiting a long time for the date to come through. You can raise any concerns you have with the Police Witness Care Unit or Citizens Advice Witness Service, who can support you as you prepare to give evidence.

If you are attending a hearing where you are not giving evidence you are entitled to read out or have read out Victim Personal statement which highlights the impact the crime has had on you, physically, psychologically or emotionally.

It might be some time since you gave your initial statement to the police. You can ask the CPS for a copy of your statement to refresh your memory before you give evidence.

You can ask to visit the court in advance of the hearing or trial to familiarise yourself with the environment. This can be helpful if you’re concerned about your proximity to the defendant or their family.

Every case is different, and you should not be dissuaded from giving evidence based on any media examples of cases or courts.

Do you need extra support?

[When you first engaged with the police at the charging decision you may have declined or not been informed of the options you have available to you for giving evidence. Special measures can be put in place to help you give your best possible evidence in Court.

These may include:

  • Giving evidence through a TV link: The witness can sit in a room outside the courtroom and give their evidence via a live television link to the courtroom.
  • Video recorded evidence: The witness' evidence is recorded and played to the court.
  • Screens around the witness box: A screen is placed around the witness box to prevent the defendant from seeing the witness.

If you are particularly worried or afraid, you can also ask the Investigating Police Officer or Witness Care Officer for extra help.

You also can read more about what Special Measures are available in the Witness Charter

Help and support

It can be very daunting preparing for Court, and it’s very normal to need to additional support. It’s vital that you reach out if you are feeling any pressure not to continue with the trial. The CPS has obligations to support you through this process.
